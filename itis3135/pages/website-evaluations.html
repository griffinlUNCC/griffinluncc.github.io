CRAP Website Evaluations

The Odin Project

The Odin Project is a croudsourced full-stack web development course, and fittingly has a well-designed webpage that serves as a strong example. It's website is aimed to encourage users to try their hand at web development, informing them of both the courses offerings and the success the course has led to.

  • Contrast
    The Odin Project's site serves as a good example for contast. For the colors, both light and dark mode use similar color schemes but are easily readable. Links and interactive elements are clearly distinguished from other text without interacting. Even text that is colored differently to emphasize itself has a similar contrast value to the rest of the text while remaining distinct from the rest of the site.

  • Repetition
    As for repetition, the Odin Project is again a great example. Elements of their webpage follow a few key patterns, and can easily be grouped with one another. Cards displaying course info always follow the same layout, paragraphs and website descriptions also are consistent and each page follows a very similar design layout. Fonts and design elements (buttons, cards, colors, stylings for shapes) are all also consistent and similar with one another in a meaningful way.

  • Alignment
    The Odin Project also implements alignment well. The pages have consistent spacing and elements are aligned with each other in such a way that your eye is able to move naturally between them. Text within elements is aligned the same way across all pages, and same with the placement of similar elements, or elements in position to one another.

  • Proximity
    The Odin Project also excels at proximity - related elements are grouped, and unrelated portions are split across different sections or pages without removing necessary information or dividing the design up significantly. White space is implemented well to both group and divide segments, and these segments flow naturally into one another.

Suzanne Collin's Books

Suzanne Collins is a well-known author most famous for the Hunger Games, so an online presence isn't necessarily crucial, and her website reflects that. Her website was made with the primary goal of informing readers aboout herself and her upcoming books, and directing readers to those books. While it accomplishes that goal well, it implements the vast majority of CRAP principles incorrectly, failing to be accessible or readable.

  • Contrast
    Startin with contrast, significant issues are already present. The color of headers, links, and emphasized text is a yellow-orange on a yellow or white background - leading to poor contrast and difficult to read text. The text itself also struggles with contrast - headings are often the same size as their subheadings, and are the same color and nearly the same size as the article text, making the information on the website difficult to parse. Interactive elements also have no distinguishing traits from the non-interactive elements - both are times new roman with a yellow-orange font color. Finally, quotes and paragraphs are written the same, leaving it difficult to parse which is which at a glance.

  • Repetition
    Suzanne Collin's site is a mixed example. While some elements are consistently placed and have a fixed layout, such as the sidebar containing links to interviews and quotes, other elements have variable spacing and content, even within the same page, leading to a difficult to parse layout. She does have a consistent color scheme and font choice, which generally (but not always) are applied to the same elements so readers can parse what has what purpose on the site at a glance.

  • Alignment
    Due to the simplicity of Collin's website, Alignment is implemented fairly well. Each page has the same structure - a left sidebar holding some relevant information, a main column holding the majority of the page's information, and a right sidebar containing a collection of links. Alignment is a bit inconsistent in some parts - depending on your viewport, elements such as the nav will be centered or uncentered. Separate content blocks are also poorly aligned - with inconsistent spacing between each paragraph and image.

  • Proximity
    As for proximity, Collin's page serves as a consistent example of what not to do - offering both clutter and too much whitespace consistently. Pages often have far too much whitespace on both sides when in desktop view, and content doesn't tend to span the whole height of the page. Paragraphs and images are often spaced apart varyingly, regardless of if they are supposed to be grouped together or not. As such, unrelated items are often close to one another, and items regarding the same topic are often split apart.
